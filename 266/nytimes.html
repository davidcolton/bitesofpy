Here’s the latest.

March 6, 2020

One week ago, Bernie Sanders had a double-digit lead in national polls. His closest opponents — Joseph R. Biden Jr., Elizabeth Warren and Michael R. Bloomberg — were bunched up in a group well behind him. The rest of the candidates, including Amy Klobuchar and Pete Buttigieg, were even further back.

Since then, the race has been transformed. Mr. Biden’s broad victories in South Carolina and on Super Tuesday have propelled him ahead of Mr. Sanders in the delegate count: On Friday morning, he led by a small but meaningful margin. Mr. Buttigieg, Ms. Klobuchar and Mr. Bloomberg all dropped out of the race to endorse Mr. Biden, while Ms. Warren ended her campaign without issuing an immediate endorsement.

With only Mr. Biden and Mr. Sanders remaining as serious contenders, Mr. Sanders will face incredible pressure in the next round of primaries to show he can compete with the coalition Mr. Biden so suddenly assembled.

Our national polling average has not caught up with any of these developments. None of the pollsters that qualify for our chart have released surveys taken since Super Tuesday, and a few polls that we do not include have suggested a drastic shift in Mr. Biden’s favor.

It remains to be seen how high Mr. Biden might rise, and whether Mr. Sanders will be able to expand his political base: So far, no Democratic candidate has assembled support from more than about a third of primary voters at any point in this campaign. Now that it is almost a binary contest, Mr. Biden or Mr. Sanders could be in a position to construct an electoral majority of primary voters pretty quickly.

If either man manages to do that, it could bring about a swifter resolution to the Democratic primary than most party leaders have been anticipating. Mr. Biden and Mr. Sanders could well split the vote more or less evenly, leading to a drawn-out campaign fought over a few undecided voters. At the moment, however, all the biggest trends are in Mr. Biden’s favor, opening the possibility — for the first time in a while — that the former vice president could establish a dominant advantage over Mr. Sanders in a matter of weeks.

— Alexander Burns

Who Is Leading the Money Race?

Presidential campaigns are expensive, and candidates’ ability to compete often depends on their prowess at collecting large sums of money. Candidates used to focus on courting a few thousand wealthy individuals; many now spend more time raising money in small increments from millions of people online.
